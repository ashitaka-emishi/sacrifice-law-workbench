# Primary Research Question

This document is a publication-facing research-design memo. Its purpose is to
tighten the project's animating question into a research question that can
support peer-reviewed scholarship, a defensible methods section, and
article-length or book-chapter claims.

## Current Problem

The current central question is powerful, but it is too large for the evidence
base in its present form:

> How does political violence convert imagined sacred objects into lived
> realities through the sacrifice of bodies, and why does this sometimes take
> the form of war while at other times it becomes genocide?

As a theoretical provocation, this is strong. As a primary research question, it
does too much at once. It risks presupposing the answer, implying broad
historical causation, and asking the corpus to explain why war and genocide
happen rather than what the selected texts can actually show.

The primary research question should be narrower, evidence-facing, and
explicitly linked to the project's methods: corpus-assisted metaphor analysis,
Conceptual Metaphor Theory, MIPVU-style lexical identification, rhetorical
criticism, and Koenigsbergian ideological-fantasy analysis.

## Recommended Primary Research Question

> To what degree do recurring metaphor systems in leader-centered political
> corpora, when compared with historically documented practices of mobilization,
> killing, dying, purification, and enemy destruction, support, complicate, or
> limit Koenigsberg's Law of Sacrifice, the body-politic corollary, and the
> construction of enemies as bringers of death across the initial pilot cases defined in the
> case-selection protocol?

This version has several advantages:

- It names the evidence: selected leader-centered political corpora.
- It names the analytical object: recurring metaphor systems.
- It avoids claiming that metaphor causes violence.
- It preserves the Koenigsbergian concern with sacred objects, sacrifice,
  bodies, enemies, and political violence.
- It defines the project as an assessment of evidentiary support rather than a
  claim of proof.
- It treats enemy as bringer of death construction as a first-class analytical dimension
  rather than as a narrow parasite/disease subtype.
- It keeps the primary question reusable as the workbench expands beyond the
  initial pilot set.
- It makes the war/genocide distinction comparative rather than causally
  overdetermined.

## Research Program Stages

The primary question is designed for a staged research program rather than a
one-time final confirmation of Koenigsberg's Law.

| Stage | Purpose | Main Output |
|---|---|---|
| Initial pilot stage | Test and calibrate the method on the initial case set defined in the case-selection protocol | Document-level and case-level support scores, scoring rubric revisions, and first comparative findings |
| Expansion stage | Add further war, genocide, revolutionary, imperial, colonial, terrorist, and state-violence cases over time | Larger comparative evidence base, revised case typologies, stronger cross-case claims, and sensitivity checks |

The pilot stage should establish whether the scoring framework is usable,
auditable, and capable of distinguishing strong, moderate, weak, complicated,
or unsupported cases. The expansion stage should test whether the same framework
continues to hold across a wider range of cases or whether Koenigsberg's Law
requires revision.

The project is evidence-led. Its purpose is not to defend Koenigsberg's Law
against the evidence, but to assess where the Law is strongly supported, where
it is only partially supported, where it requires revision, and where it fails.
Expansion cases should be allowed to complicate, limit, or reject the theory.

## Evidentiary Support Standard

This project does not claim to prove Koenigsberg's Law of Sacrifice in a final
or deductive sense. It assesses the degree to which each case supports,
complicates, limits, or fails to support the Law and its body-politic corollary.

Support is assessed across four core dimensions:

1. sacred object;
2. sacrificial body;
3. enemy as bringer of death;
4. historical enactment or alignment.

Support is strongest when several forms of evidence converge:

1. recurring metaphor systems construct a sacred collective object;
2. bodies are represented as sacrificial, redeeming, purifying, indebted,
   expendable, or necessary to the object's survival;
3. killing and dying are represented as acts that preserve, rescue, confirm, or
   make real the sacred object;
4. the enemy is represented as death-bearing for the sacred object: an agent, carrier,
   embodiment, or sign of doubt, mortality, dissolution, unreality, or
   collective destruction that must be expelled, defeated, sacrificed, or
   destroyed;
5. these textual-symbolic patterns align with historically documented
   practices of mobilization, killing, dying, purification, exclusion, or enemy
   destruction.

Case-level findings should be classified as strong support, moderate support,
weak support, complicated support, or unsupported.

## Support Rating Framework

Each case should receive both dimension-level ratings and an overall support
rating. The dimension-level ratings come first; the overall rating is a
synthesis of those scores and their evidentiary pattern.

Use an anchored 0-4 scale rather than a 0-100 scale as the primary scholarly
rating. A 0-100 score implies a level of measurement precision that the evidence
cannot support at this stage. If useful for dashboards or later visualization,
the 0-4 scores can be normalized to 0-100, but the publication-facing analysis
should use the anchored scale and qualitative category.

| Score | Rating | Meaning |
|---:|---|---|
| 0 | Unsupported | No meaningful evidence for the dimension in the case material |
| 1 | Weak support | Isolated, ambiguous, or low-confidence evidence |
| 2 | Moderate support | Repeated evidence, but limited by scope, ambiguity, or weak historical alignment |
| 3 | Strong support | Repeated, traceable, and contextually persuasive evidence |
| 4 | Very strong support | Dense, repeated, cross-textual evidence with clear historical alignment |

Rate each case on four dimensions:

| Dimension | Guiding Question |
|---|---|
| Sacred object | Is a collective object represented as transcendent, ultimate, sacred, immortal, or worth dying/killing for? |
| Sacrificial body | Are bodies represented as meaningful offerings, payments, instruments, victims, martyrs, or material through which the sacred object is preserved or made real? |
| Enemy as bringer of death | Is the enemy represented as the agent, carrier, embodiment, or sign of death, doubt, mortality, dissolution, unreality, or collective destruction? |
| Historical enactment or alignment | Do the textual-symbolic patterns align with historically documented practices, policies, institutions, mobilizations, or outcomes? |

Suggested interpretation for the sacred object score:

| Score | Sacred Object |
|---:|---|
| 0 | No collective object is represented as sacred, ultimate, transcendent, immortal, or worth sacrifice |
| 1 | A possible sacred object appears, but evidence is isolated, ambiguous, conventional, or low-confidence |
| 2 | A sacred object recurs, but the pattern is limited to one text, one genre, or weakly developed entailments |
| 3 | A sacred object is repeated across texts or passages and is clearly linked to duty, obligation, identity, survival, or moral value |
| 4 | A sacred object is dense, recurring, cross-textual, and central to the case's symbolic organization of violence |

Suggested interpretation for the sacrificial body score:

| Score | Sacrificial Body |
|---:|---|
| 0 | Bodies, suffering, killing, or dying are not represented as meaningful for the sacred object |
| 1 | Isolated or ambiguous references connect bodies, suffering, killing, or dying to collective meaning |
| 2 | Repeated references connect bodies, suffering, killing, or dying to duty, payment, honor, redemption, preservation, or renewal, but the pattern is limited or weakly integrated |
| 3 | Bodies are clearly represented as offerings, payments, instruments, martyrs, victims, or material through which the sacred object is preserved or made real |
| 4 | Sacrificial bodies are central, repeated, cross-textual, and explicitly tied to the reality, survival, renewal, purification, or immortality of the sacred object |

Suggested interpretation for the enemy as bringer of death score:

| Score | Enemy as Bringer of Death |
|---:|---|
| 0 | The enemy is not represented as death-bearing, dissolving, unrealizing, contaminating, or existentially dangerous |
| 1 | Isolated or ambiguous enemy-threat imagery appears, but the enemy remains mostly an ordinary opponent |
| 2 | The enemy is repeatedly represented as dangerous to the sacred object or collective body, but the death-bearing function is limited, conventional, or weakly developed |
| 3 | The enemy is clearly represented as an agent, carrier, embodiment, or sign of collective death, doubt, dissolution, contamination, enslavement, desecration, or mortality |
| 4 | Enemy-as-bringer-of-death evidence is dense, repeated, cross-textual, and central to the justification of sacrifice, purification, exclusion, or destruction |

Scores of `3` or `4` in the three textual-symbolic dimensions require repeated
evidence. A single rhetorically central passage may be marked as
high-salience evidence, but it should not by itself establish strong or very
strong case-level support. A score of `3` requires recurrence across multiple
passages or sections. A score of `4` requires dense recurrence across multiple
texts, phases, genres, or rhetorically central locations. If a case contains one
extraordinary passage but little recurrence, record that as a salient exception
rather than inflating the dimension score.

## Document-Level And Case-Level Scoring

The project should score support at both the document level and the case level.
Document-level scores preserve the evidence trail. Case-level scores provide the
publishable synthesis.

Each document receives scores for the relevant dimensions:

```text
document_score = S, B, E, H
```

Where `S`, `B`, `E`, and `H` retain the same 0-4 anchors. Historical enactment
or alignment may be `0` for documents that are purely rhetorical and do not
directly document policy, implementation, social uptake, or historical outcome.

Case-level dimension scores should be derived from document-level scores using
a transparent weighted mean:

```text
case_dimension_score = sum(document_score * document_weight) / sum(document_weight)
```

Document weights should be assigned before scoring interpretation, using
predefined metadata such as:

- source reliability;
- genre relevance;
- rhetorical centrality;
- historical importance;
- case-design priority;
- whether the document belongs to the balanced core or extended corpus.

Weights should be modest and auditable, for example:

| Weight | Meaning |
|---:|---|
| 1.0 | Standard case document |
| 1.5 | High-priority or rhetorically central document |
| 2.0 | Canonical, policy-defining, or historically decisive document |

No single document should receive enough weight to override the recurrence
requirement for strong or very strong textual-symbolic scores. A rhetorically
central document can raise the case score, but the case-level explanation must
state whether support is broadly distributed or concentrated in a few documents.

Document weights should be derived through a hybrid review workflow:

1. use existing metadata fields such as analytical priority, genre, phase,
   source reliability, authorship confidence, translation risk, source risk,
   balanced-core status, and historical role to generate an initial proposed
   weight;
2. use agentic scholarly review to check consistency across cases, flag
   anomalies, and explain why each proposed weight is appropriate;
3. require manual human review for high-risk or high-impact weighting decisions.

Manual review is required when:

- a document is assigned weight `2.0`;
- a document materially changes a case-level support rating;
- source reliability, authorship, translation, OCR quality, or provenance is
  uncertain;
- a document serves both as textual-symbolic evidence and historical enactment
  evidence;
- the document concerns genocide, extermination, racial ideology, enslavement,
  or other ethically high-stakes material;
- the proposed score or weight is an outlier relative to comparable documents.

This workflow is more defensible than either fully manual or fully automated
weighting. Metadata-derived weighting improves consistency and reproducibility.
Agentic scholarly review improves explanation and anomaly detection. Human
review preserves accountability for high-risk interpretive decisions.

The historical enactment or alignment dimension should assess how far the
symbolic pattern travels into historical reality:

| Historical Level | Description |
|---|---|
| Formal enactment | A policy, order, decree, law, proclamation, institutional statement, or official program expresses or authorizes the relevant pattern |
| Implementation | Institutions, armies, bureaucracies, parties, courts, police, or other agents carry out practices aligned with the pattern |
| Social uptake | Soldiers, citizens, officials, party members, local actors, or wider publics participate in, comply with, internalize, repeat, resist, or materially respond to the pattern |
| Historical consequence | The aligned practice produces durable, documented outcomes such as mobilization, killing, exclusion, purification, extermination, martyrdom, or collective sacrifice |

Suggested interpretation for the historical enactment or alignment score:

| Score | Historical Enactment Or Alignment |
|---:|---|
| 0 | No established historical alignment |
| 1 | Plausible but weak or poorly corroborated alignment |
| 2 | Formal enactment or well-established contextual alignment |
| 3 | Formal enactment plus corroborated implementation |
| 4 | Corroborated implementation plus social uptake or durable historical consequence |

Overall support should be calculated with a weighted shifted geometric mean:

```text
overall_support = ((S + 1) * (B + 1) * (E + 1) * (H + 1)^2)^(1/5) - 1
```

Where:

- `S` = sacred object score;
- `B` = sacrificial body score;
- `E` = enemy as bringer of death score;
- `H` = historical enactment or alignment score.

The result remains on the same 0-4 scale. This formula is preferable to a
simple arithmetic average because support for Koenigsberg's Law is conjunctive.
The theory is strongest when all four dimensions appear together. A high score
in one dimension should not fully compensate for the absence of another. The
shift by `+1` prevents one missing dimension from automatically reducing the
entire case to zero, while still penalizing uneven evidence.

Historical enactment or alignment receives double weight because it is the
historical-anchoring dimension. The textual-symbolic dimensions can show that a
corpus is Koenigsbergian in structure, but historical alignment is what connects
that structure to actual practices of mobilization, killing, dying,
purification, exclusion, or enemy destruction. Without historical alignment, the
project can claim a strong textual reading, but not strong case-level support
for the Law.

Because historical alignment functions as a corroborating check, apply a
category cap after calculating the overall support index:

| Historical enactment or alignment score | Maximum overall category |
|---:|---|
| 0 | Weak support |
| 1 | Weak support |
| 2 | Moderate support |
| 3 | Strong support |
| 4 | Very strong support |

For publication-facing prose, report the overall support value to one decimal
place, followed by a qualitative category and a short explanation. Do not treat
the score as a probability or proof value. It is a structured support index.

Optional dashboard value:

```text
support_0_to_100 = overall_support * 25
```

The 0-100 value may be useful for visualization, but the 0-4 anchored score is
the primary scholarly rating.

Overall support should be reported as both a category and a short explanation:

| Overall Pattern | Category |
|---|---|
| Overall support 3.25-4.00, with no dimension below 2 | Very strong support |
| Overall support 2.50-3.24, with no dimension below 2 | Strong support |
| Overall support 1.75-2.49, or one dimension below 2 | Moderate support |
| Overall support 1.00-1.74, or multiple dimensions below 2 | Weak support |
| Overall support below 1.00 | Unsupported |

Use a "complicated support" note when the support score is misleading because
one or more dimensions strongly support the Law while another dimension resists,
contradicts, or reframes it.

## Sharper Alternative Versions

Use one of these if the project needs a more focused article, chapter, or
case-study frame.

### Corpus-First Version

> What recurring metaphor systems organize sacred collective objects,
> sacrificial bodies, enemies as bringers of death, and violence in selected
> leader-centered political corpora, and how do those systems align with
> historically documented practices across case, historical phase, genre, and
> war/genocide context?

This is the safest version methodologically. It foregrounds corpus patterns and
allows interpretation to emerge after annotation.

### Theory-Testing Version

> To what extent do selected leader-centered political corpora support
> Koenigsberg's claim that political violence sacralizes collective objects
> through the sacrifice of bodies, and where do textual patterns and
> historically documented practices complicate, limit, or contradict that
> claim?

This version is useful if the article is explicitly framed as a test,
revision, or qualification of Koenigsberg's Law of Sacrifice.

### War/Genocide Distinction Version

> How do metaphor systems of sacrifice, sacred collectivity, body-politic
> threat, enemy as bringer of death construction, and enemy destruction differ
> between war-oriented and genocidal political rhetoric in the selected corpora?

This is the best version if the project's central contribution is the
distinction between war and genocide.

### Lincoln-Focused Version

> In Lincoln's selected public texts, how do body-politic, covenantal,
> providential, and sacrificial metaphors organize the relation among Union,
> death, guilt, and national renewal across 1838-1865?

This version should be used if Lincoln becomes the main case rather than one
case within a cross-case study.

## Secondary Research Questions

1. What metaphor-related lexical units recur across the selected corpora, and
   which conceptual mappings do they support?
2. Which sacred collective objects are constructed in each case: nation, Union,
   liberty, empire, race, revolution, army, law, providence, or another object?
3. How are bodies represented: as offerings, wounds, instruments, extensions of
   the collective, contaminants, parasites, martyrs, debts, or redeemed matter?
4. How are enemies represented as bringers of death for the sacred object: as external
   opponents, internal enemies, parasites, diseases, poisons, contaminants,
   criminals, traitors, lawbreakers, desecrators, tyrants, enslavers,
   aggressors, demonic forces, obstacles to destiny, embodiments of doubt, or
   imagined sources of collective death?
5. What forms of violence are made morally necessary, obligatory, redemptive,
   defensive, purifying, or inevitable?
6. How do metaphor systems vary across genre, register, historical phase, and
   audience?
7. What absences or suppressions matter: civilian suffering, enslaved people,
   enemy subjectivity, perpetrator agency, coercion, contingency, or failed
   sacrifice?
8. What differences distinguish war-making rhetoric from genocidal rhetoric in
   enemy construction, body imagery, purification logic, sacrifice logic, and
   imagined exit conditions?
9. Where do the corpora resist, complicate, or falsify Koenigsbergian
   expectations?
10. Where do textual patterns align with historically documented practices,
    policies, institutions, mobilizations, or outcomes?

## Working Thesis

The project should not begin by asserting that political violence converts
fantasy into reality. It should begin by testing whether selected political
corpora repeatedly organize violence through metaphor systems that sacralize
collective objects, assign value to sacrificial bodies, construct enemies as
death-bearing threats to the collective body, and define violence as necessary,
redemptive, or purifying.

A provisional thesis might be:

> Selected leader-centered political corpora repeatedly organize violence
> through metaphor systems that join sacred collective objects to sacrificial
> bodies. These textual-symbolic patterns can be tested against historically
> documented practices of mobilization, killing, dying, purification, exclusion,
> and enemy destruction. War-oriented rhetoric tends to emphasize obligation,
> endurance, redemption, and collective renewal, while genocidal rhetoric more
> strongly emphasizes contamination, purification, enemy embodiment, impossible
> coexistence, and enemy as bringer of death construction. These distinctions are not
> absolute, but they provide a testable framework for comparing how political
> language gives violence moral necessity.

This should remain provisional until annotation and scholarly review are
complete.

## Key Terms To Operationalize

| Term | Working Definition | Evidence Needed |
|---|---|---|
| Sacred collective object | A political object treated as transcendent, ultimate, holy, immortal, providential, or worth dying/killing for | Metaphor clusters, evaluative language, appeals to duty, covenant, destiny, purity, immortality, or sacred obligation |
| Sacrificial body | A body whose injury, death, suffering, or exposure to violence is represented as meaningful for the collective object | Lexical units and mappings involving blood, death, offering, martyrdom, debt, payment, birth, renewal, purification, or redemption |
| Enemy as bringer of death | A Koenigsbergian label for an enemy represented not merely as an opponent, but as the agent, carrier, embodiment, or sign of death for the sacred object. The enemy may embody doubt that the sacred object is real, knowledge that the collective body is mortal, or a force imagined to bring dissolution, contamination, enslavement, desecration, or collective destruction. | Metaphors or frames of disease, parasite, poison, contamination, criminality, treason, lawbreaking, desecration, enslavement, tyranny, aggression, demonic threat, internal subversion, existential danger, doubt, mortality, unreality, or collective death |
| Metaphor system | A repeated and internally related pattern of source-target mappings, entailments, and rhetorical functions | MIPVU-coded lexical units, CMT mappings, cluster profiles, concordance patterns, and close readings |
| Morally necessary violence | Violence represented as duty, defense, purification, sacrifice, justice, providence, destiny, or unavoidable historical action | Rhetorical action codes, modal language, obligation terms, enemy construction, and justification sequences |
| War-making context | Rhetoric oriented toward armed conflict, military sacrifice, enemy defeat, survival, honor, law, or collective preservation | Case metadata, genre, phase, military or political register, and explicit references to war |
| Genocidal context | Rhetoric oriented toward group destruction, elimination, purification, racial threat, permanent enemy embodiment, or impossible coexistence | Enemy as bringer of death mappings, purification logic, exterminatory language, and historical case context |
| Historical enactment or alignment | Contextual historical evidence used to test whether textual-symbolic patterns align with documented practices or outcomes | Policies, institutions, mobilization practices, military or bureaucratic records, killings, exclusions, exterminatory measures, and established historical scholarship |

## Role Of Historical Evidence

Historical evidence is used for context and corroboration, not as a free-standing
claim that metaphor alone causes policy or violence. The project asks whether
recurring metaphor systems align with historically documented practices of
mobilization, killing, dying, purification, exclusion, or extermination.

Historical enactment or alignment should rely primarily on major policies, practices,
institutions, and outcomes that are already well established in the relevant
scholarship. The workbench should not try to produce a new archival history for
each case. If a proposed historical alignment is not well established, the gap
should be marked explicitly rather than filled by speculation.

Primary sources already in the corpus may also count as historical enactment or
alignment evidence when they directly document policy, institutional practice,
orders, mobilization, exclusion, killing, purification, or enemy-destruction
measures. In such cases, the same document can provide both textual-symbolic
evidence and historical evidence, but the two uses must be coded separately.
Otherwise the project risks circular reasoning: treating a metaphor as
historically corroborated merely because it appears in a policy text.

When one document serves both roles, record:

1. the textual-symbolic claim it supports;
2. the policy, order, practice, or institutional action it documents;
3. whether the historical practice is corroborated by established secondary
   scholarship or by additional primary evidence.

Historical enactment or alignment scores of `3` or `4` require corroboration.
A primary policy, order, decree, proclamation, or institutional document by
itself can support a historical alignment score up to `2`. To score `3` or `4`,
the documented practice must also be corroborated by established secondary
scholarship, additional primary evidence, implementation records, institutional
practice, or well-established historical outcomes.

This dimension measures the extent to which a textual-symbolic fantasy is
historically enacted. It should not be treated as proof of a leader's private
mental state. It asks whether a recurring symbolic pattern remains rhetorical,
is formally authorized, is implemented by institutions, is taken up socially, or
produces durable historical consequences.

The preferred claim form is:

> Metaphor pattern A aligns with historically documented practice B.

The project should avoid the stronger unsupported form:

> Metaphor pattern A caused historically documented practice B.

## What The Project Can Claim

The project can credibly claim:

- that specific metaphor-related lexical units occur in specific passages;
- that those units support specific conceptual mappings;
- that mappings cluster into recurring metaphor systems;
- that those systems vary across case, phase, genre, and register;
- that specific texts construct enemies as death-bearing threats rather than
  ordinary opponents;
- that some metaphor systems make violence appear obligatory, redemptive,
  defensive, purifying, or historically necessary;
- that textual-symbolic patterns align with specific documented practices,
  policies, institutions, or outcomes;
- that war-oriented and genocidal corpora differ in patterned ways;
- that Koenigsberg's theory is supported, revised, or limited by the evidence.
- that some cases may fail to support Koenigsberg's Law or may require a
  revised version of it.

The project should not claim, without additional historical evidence:

- that metaphor alone causes war or genocide;
- that a leader's private unconscious state has been proven;
- that political violence has one cause;
- that all sacrificial rhetoric is equivalent;
- that war and genocide differ by a single linguistic feature;
- that selected leader-centered corpora represent an entire society.

## Clarifying Questions For Research Design

These are the questions to answer before treating the research question as
publication-ready.

1. Is the project primarily about Koenigsberg's theory, about metaphor in
   political violence, or about the distinction between war and genocide?
2. Is Lincoln the central case, a test case, or one case among several?
3. Should the project's main contribution be theoretical,
   methodological, historical, or comparative?
4. What must the project demonstrate for the central claim to be publishable?
5. What would count as evidence against Koenigsberg's Law of Sacrifice?
6. Are sacred objects always unconscious fantasies in your argument, or can they
   also be publicly available rhetorical constructions?
7. Are you studying political violence itself, or the symbolic systems through
   which political violence is represented and justified?
8. Should the project treat war and genocide as distinct phenomena, overlapping
   sacrificial forms, or points on a continuum?
9. What is the smallest defensible corpus that could answer the primary
   question?
10. Should the project compare leaders, regimes, movements, genres, or
    historical situations?
11. How much weight should be placed on frequency and distribution, and how
    much on close reading of rhetorically decisive passages?
12. Which cases are indispensable, and which are optional?
13. What historical scholarship must the project satisfy in each case?
14. What are the ethical risks of comparing Lincoln, Napoleon, revolutionary
    America, and Hitler under one sacrificial framework?
15. How will the project avoid making morally flattening comparisons
    between democratic war rhetoric, imperial war rhetoric, revolutionary
    rhetoric, and genocidal rhetoric?
16. What is the role of absence analysis: supplement, central contribution, or
    validation check?
17. Will the final claim be that Koenigsberg is right, partly right, or useful
    but in need of methodological repair?
18. What kind of publishable project should this be: a theory article with
    textual evidence, a corpus-methods article with theory, or a
    historical-rhetorical study with computational support?

## Immediate Decisions Needed

Before annotation becomes interpretation, the project should decide:

1. Whether the primary research question will be cross-case or Lincoln-centered.
2. Whether the main dependent contrast is war/genocide, sacrifice/purification,
   or sacred object/enemy body.
3. Whether Koenigsberg's Law of Sacrifice is being applied, tested, revised, or
   reconstructed.
4. Whether "leader-centered corpus" is the final corpus model or a temporary
   V1 convenience.
5. Whether the project will foreground metaphor identification,
   ideological-fantasy interpretation, or historical comparison.

## Recommended Next Move

Use the recommended primary research question as the working version for now,
but answer the clarifying questions before treating it as final. The question
should be revisited after the first complete annotated case because the corpus
may reveal that the strongest publishable argument is narrower than the current
cross-case ambition.
