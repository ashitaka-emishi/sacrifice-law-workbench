# Critical Metaphor Analysis: napoleon

Status: draft, CMT-backed interpretive layer.

## napoleon-01-glory-destiny

Mapping IDs: napoleon-cmt-003, napoleon-cmt-006, napoleon-cmt-007, napoleon-cmt-008, napoleon-cmt-015, napoleon-cmt-017

Persuasive function: Makes military victory feel like historical destiny, converting contingent battle outcomes into inevitable providential unfolding.

Relation to Koenigsbergian analysis: Naturalizes the Emperor as the sacred object's embodiment; destiny suppresses the contingency and human agency behind the wars.

Rival readings:

- Glory is not a physical covering or companion, but the French phrasing treats it as something one is covered with or dies with.
- The objects may be literal, but they also condense monarchy, victory, and imperial memory.

Negative cases:

- Glory/destiny framing is most intense in the early victories (Austerlitz, Jena); after Eylau and especially after 1812 it becomes strained.

## napoleon-02-army-body

Mapping IDs: napoleon-cmt-001, napoleon-cmt-005, napoleon-cmt-009, napoleon-cmt-010, napoleon-cmt-013, napoleon-cmt-014, napoleon-cmt-016, napoleon-cmt-018

Persuasive function: Constructs the Grande Armée as a collective organism whose health and discipline embody the Empire's vitality.

Relation to Koenigsbergian analysis: Supports the sacrificial-body dimension: the army's suffering is the body whose sacrifice sustains the imperial order.

Rival readings:

- Cannons are not literal mouths; the French military term preserves body/fire imagery.
- Fortune does not literally smile or act like a human force.
- The army's honor is abstract while blood is bodily; the pair turns battle into bodily and reputational expenditure.
- Victory is abstract but is described as hesitating, deciding, or belonging to an army.

Negative cases:

- Army-as-body language is more prominent in bulletins with high casualty counts (Eylau); it functions to absorb individual loss into collective identity.

## napoleon-03-emperor-embodiment

Mapping IDs: napoleon-cmt-002

Persuasive function: Consolidates authority by making Napoleon the personal embodiment of France, history, and destiny.

Relation to Koenigsbergian analysis: The Emperor is the sacred object and its guardian simultaneously; sacrifice for Napoleon is sacrifice for the nation made flesh.

Rival readings:

- The deaths are literal, but the office and thanksgiving frame them as meaningful patriotic-sacred sacrifice.

Negative cases:

- Emperor-embodiment is an imperial construction; the bulletins do not record dissent, desertion, or the limits of the soldiers' actual devotion.

## napoleon-05-soldier-sacrifice

Mapping IDs: napoleon-cmt-004, napoleon-cmt-011, napoleon-cmt-012, napoleon-cmt-019

Persuasive function: Converts soldier death into honor, glory, and immortality, making mass casualties rhetorically sustainable.

Relation to Koenigsbergian analysis: Directly activates the sacrificial-body dimension: soldier death as meaningful offering sustaining the imperial sacred object.

Rival readings:

- A battle does not literally possess destiny, but the outcome is construed as fate-like.
- Some death is literal, but the phrasing treats death as an output, seed, or glory-bearing sacrificial cost.

Negative cases:

- The Eylau bulletin is the clearest case; after the Russian disaster the same framing strains credibility and disappears from the 29th Bulletin.
